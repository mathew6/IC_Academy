Basics of an Ellipse

"The orbit of every planet is an ellipse with the sun at a focus." Johannes Kepler exclaimed after observing Mars for several years and tracking its motion around the sun. Similar to planets traveling around the sun, satalleties also orbit the earth in elliptical paths. Lets first go over the make up of an ellipse:

Name Definition Key
Origin Center point of ellipses.
Foci Two fixed points on the interior of an ellipse used in the formal definition of the curve.
Semi-Major Axis The longest possible radius of an elliptical orbit
Semi-Minor Axis The shortest possible radius of an elliptical orbit

As demonstrated above, the satellite (green moving circle) is traveling around the Earth which is always one of the foci invloved in elliptical orbits. The other focus (also referred to as the vacant focus) is another point in space symmetrical to the Earth just on the opposite side of the ellipse. We don't have to worry about the vacant focus but it is important to understand that an ellipse is made up of two foci. The Semi-Major axis is easily remembered as the longest possible radius from the origin, where as the Semi-Minor axis is the shortest possible radius from the center. A circle is a special type of an ellipse where the Semi-Major and Semi-Minor axis are equal to eachother. In fact, the infinite amount of radii found within a circle are equal to eachother. Perfect symmetry :) !

Eccentricity

Next let's take a look at Eccentricity. Review the defintions below and observe the visual above:

Name Definition Key
Eccentricity The distance between eithier foci and the origin divided by the semi-major axis.
Origin Center point of ellipses.
Foci Two fixed points on the interior of an ellipse used in the formal definition of the curve.
Semi-Major Axis The radius of an orbit at the orbit's two most distant points
Semi-Minor Axis The radius of an orbit at the orbit's two nearest points

Eccentricity defines the shape or type of conic section. It is the ratio of the distance between each foci (2c) over the major axis (2a). Because the distance between the foci in an ellipse is always less than the length of the ellipse (aka the major axis), its eccentricity will always be between 0 and 1. Apogee and Perigee

Apogee and Perigee

Next let's take a look at Apogee and Perigee. Review the defintions below and observe the visual above:

Name Definition Key
Apogee The point in the orbit of a satellite at which it is farthest to the earth.
Perigee The point in the orbit of a satellite at which it is nearest to the earth.
Foci Two fixed points on the interior of an ellipse used in the formal definition of the curve.
Satellite The object orbiting Earth in an elliptical path.

The Earth is rarely located on the origin of an ellipse, therefore a satellite usually has a point of minimum distance relative to the earth and a point of maximum distance. The point where the sattellite is furthest away is known as the Apogee of the orbit and when it is at its closest, this is known as the Perogee.
